Iceberg vs. Plain Parquet: When a Table Format Earns Its Keep

July 2026 · 6 min read

A folder of Parquet files gets you shockingly far. Partition by date, point DuckDB or Spark at a glob path, and you have a query-able "table" for the cost of nothing. Plenty of teams run real workloads this way, and for a while it's the right call. Then one of four things happens, and you discover what a table format is actually for.

The four things

1. A write fails halfway. Your job dies after writing six of eleven files. With plain Parquet, readers now see a partial batch - and there's no marker saying so. You end up inventing _SUCCESS flags or staging-then-rename choreography, badly reinventing what Iceberg's snapshots give you for free: readers see the last committed state, entire batches appear atomically or not at all.

2. The schema changes. Upstream adds a column. Old files don't have it; new files do. Some engines shrug and null-fill; others error at plan time; one memorable failure mode silently maps columns by position. Iceberg tracks schema by field ID, so add/rename/drop are metadata operations with defined behavior across every file, old and new.

3. You need to delete specific rows. A duplicate batch, a GDPR request, a bad backfill. With bare files your unit of surgery is the file, rewrite everything containing the offending rows, and hope nothing reads mid-rewrite. Iceberg gives you row-level deletes and, since readers pin a snapshot, time travel back to before your mistake. That last one has turned a would-be incident into a one-liner for a lot of people.

4. Small files pile up. Frequent appends produce thousands of tiny files, and query planning starts drowning in listing overhead. Iceberg's manifests replace directory listing with metadata pruning, and compaction is a maintenance command instead of a bespoke script you wrote at 11pm.

The honest case against

Iceberg adds a catalog to run, a metadata layer to understand, and a new failure vocabulary (orphaned manifests, snapshot expiry) to learn. If your data is append-only, single-writer, schema-stable, and batch-loaded once a day, a lot of analytics honestly is, plain Parquet plus discipline is simpler and fine. The format earns its keep when writers multiply, schemas drift, or correctness matters more than convenience.

A cheap way to build the intuition

In my transit lakehouse project, live subway positions land in an Iceberg table every minute, a workload with exactly the failure modes above: a poller that can die mid-write, duplicate entities needing dedup, and small files arriving constantly. Running it for a week teaches you more about why table formats exist than any comparison post, including this one.
