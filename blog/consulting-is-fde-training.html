Consulting Is Secretly FDE Training

July 2026 · 5 min read

There's a stock piece of career advice that says consulting is where engineering skills go to atrophy, too many slide decks, not enough code. I spent three years doing data work on client accounts before moving toward product and startup work, and I think the advice gets it backwards, at least for one career path. If you want to be a forward-deployed engineer, client work is close to ideal preparation.

What transfers directly

Owning problems in someone else's house. On a client account, nothing is yours: not the infrastructure, not the data, not the access process, not the change-approval board. You learn to get things done through systems you don't control and can't fully see. That is the FDE job description almost verbatim.

Escalation calls. When a production pipeline breaks and the client's Monday reporting depends on it, you end up on a call defending a diagnosis to people who are stressed, senior, and not engineers. Doing that a few dozen times builds a specific muscle: staying precise while someone important is unhappy. No amount of internal sprint work builds it.

Requirements archaeology. Clients rarely tell you what they need; they tell you what the last vendor built. Learning to interview a subject-matter expert, to ask "walk me through the last time this number looked wrong" instead of "what are your requirements", is the highest-leverage skill I took from that work. FDE interviews test for exactly this, usually with a role-play.

Being the only continuous person. Client teams rotate. If you stay on an account long enough, you become the institutional memory - the one who knows why the pipeline has that weird filter. FDEs occupy the same seat between a rotating product org and a rotating customer org.

What consulting will not teach you

Two things, and they're the reason the transition still takes work.

Product taste. In consulting, the client's word is final - if they want the dashboard, you build the dashboard. An FDE is supposed to push back on behalf of the product: to say "you don't want a custom field here, you want the standard workflow, and here's why." That instinct, knowing when the customer is wrong about their own request, has to be learned on the product side of the table.

Code that outlives you. Consulting deliverables are judged at handover. Product code is judged three years later by whoever inherits it. The engineering bar for "done" is different, and consultants moving to FDE roles usually feel that gap in their first code review.

If you're making the jump

Frame your client stories as deployment stories: what was ambiguous, what you shipped, what broke, who you had to convince. Skip the methodology vocabulary, nobody hiring FDEs wants to hear about delivery frameworks. And build one thing end-to-end in public, however small, to show the product muscle exists. The client-side scar tissue is the rare half of the profile; the code is the provable half. You need both on the table.
