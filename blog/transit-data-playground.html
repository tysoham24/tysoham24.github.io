Public Transit Data Is the Best Free Playground for Data Engineers

July 2026 · 5 min read

Every data engineering portfolio has the same three projects: a Kaggle CSV loaded into Postgres, a Twitter, sorry, X, sentiment pipeline that stopped working when the API got paywalled, and a "streaming" demo that streams a file from disk. Recruiters have seen all of them a hundred times, and none of them teach you the thing that actually makes pipeline work hard: a live source you don't control.

Transit feeds fix this. Most big transit agencies publish GTFS-Realtime - a protobuf feed of live vehicle positions, trip updates, and alerts. New York's MTA publishes the entire subway system, no API key, no rate-limit dance, no credit card. Every minute of every day, a few hundred trains report where they are. It's the most production-like data you can get without a job.

What it teaches that CSVs can't

Protobuf and schemas you don't own. Your first hour is reading someone else's schema definition and figuring out which of the optional fields are actually populated. That's not friction, that's the job. Half of data engineering is archaeology on other people's data models.

Late, missing, and weird data, for real. Trains lose GPS in tunnels. Feed headers lag. Entities disappear mid-trip and come back with a different ID. You don't have to simulate messiness; the source supplies it, and your pipeline either handles it or falls over visibly. Deduplication, watermarks, and freshness checks stop being interview vocabulary and start being things you added because Tuesday broke.

Time-series questions with real answers. Once positions are landing, headways fall out naturally: how long between trains at this stop, on this line, at rush hour versus midnight? You can check your numbers against your own commute. Data you can feel is data you'll debug properly.

The shape of a good project

Mine is open on GitHub: a poller decodes the feed and appends to an Iceberg table, quality gates refuse stale or duplicate batches, Dagster runs the schedule, and dbt builds headway marts you can query in DuckDB. Nothing in it is exotic, that's the point. It's small enough to read in an evening and real enough that every component earns its place.

Whatever stack you pick, aim for the same properties: a live source, append-only raw storage, explicit quality checks, and one genuinely interesting query at the end. And run it for a week before you call it done. Day one always works. It's day four, when the agency pushes a schema change, or a feed goes down for maintenance at 3 a.m., that turns a demo into an actual system, and gives you the story interviewers are fishing for when they ask "tell me about a time something broke."
