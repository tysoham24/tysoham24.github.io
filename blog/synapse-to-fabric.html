Synapse to Fabric: What Actually Changes

July 2026 · 7 min read

Microsoft's positioning makes Fabric sound like Synapse with a new coat of paint, which leads teams to scope the migration as a lift-and-shift. It isn't one. Three separate things change underneath you, the storage and compute model, the pricing model, and the BI serving path, and each one invalidates a habit that Synapse taught you.

1. The warehouse stops being a database

A Synapse dedicated SQL pool is a classic MPP warehouse: data lives inside the engine, and you spend your tuning effort on distribution strategy - hash-distributing big facts, replicating small dimensions, praying you picked the right key before the 2 TB table finished loading.

In Fabric, everything lands in OneLake as Delta tables, and engines (warehouse, lakehouse SQL endpoint, Spark) attach to the same files. Two practical consequences:

First, distribution keys stop existing. The tuning vocabulary becomes file-oriented: Delta table maintenance, compaction, V-Order. If your Synapse performance depended on carefully chosen hash distributions, you need to re-verify every big join, not assume parity.

Second, T-SQL surface area shrinks in the lakehouse SQL endpoint. Multi-statement stored procedures full of temp tables, identity columns, cross-database references, the workhorse patterns of a decade of warehouse ETL, need triage: some port directly to the Fabric warehouse, some want rewriting as Spark or dataflows, and a few should honestly just die. Budget the triage before promising a timeline. This is where migrations slip.

2. Capacity pricing changes your operational habits

Synapse dedicated pools bill by DWU: a fixed-size engine you scale deliberately and pause at night. Fabric bills by capacity units shared across everything, pipelines, Spark, warehouse queries, Power BI. It's one pool of compute with smoothing and, when you exhaust it, throttling that users experience as "reports are slow."

The habit change: in Synapse you tuned queries; in Fabric you also have to budget workloads against each other. A greedy backfill can starve the CEO's dashboard. The capacity metrics app becomes a first-class operational tool, not an afterthought, put someone on it from day one, and run a week of production-shaped load before you size the capacity SKU, not after.

3. Direct Lake changes the BI contract

This is the part that actually justifies the migration. DirectQuery gave you freshness at the cost of per-visual SQL round-trips; Import gave you speed at the cost of refresh windows. Direct Lake reads Delta files into memory straight from OneLake, near-Import speed with no scheduled refresh.

But it comes with conditions. Models can fall back to DirectQuery when you exceed guardrails (row counts, memory limits, certain unsupported features), and the fallback is silent unless you monitor for it, your "fast" dashboard quietly becomes a slow one after a table doubles. Table maintenance matters too: Direct Lake performance tracks how well-compacted and V-Ordered your Delta files are, which means your ingestion pipelines and your BI performance are now coupled. That coupling is new; in the Synapse world those were different teams' problems.

How I'd sequence it

Move the data first and keep the old serving path alive: land everything as Delta in OneLake, mirror or dual-write during the transition. Then migrate consumption, semantic models to Direct Lake, validating fallback behavior per model. Migrate transformation logic last, because it's the slowest and the data/BI wins don't depend on it. Teams that start with the stored-procedure rewrite spend six months with nothing to show stakeholders; teams that start with Direct Lake dashboards have a visible win in the first month while the grind proceeds behind it.

The migration is worth it for most Synapse estates, dedicated pools are clearly not where Microsoft's investment is going. Just don't let anyone scope it as a rename.
