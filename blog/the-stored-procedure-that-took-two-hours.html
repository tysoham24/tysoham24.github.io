The Stored Procedure That Took Two Hours

July 2026 · 5 min read

Early in my consulting years I inherited a stored procedure that built a fact table for a client's nightly load. It ran for a bit over two hours. Nobody knew why. It had always taken two hours, the way a commute always takes an hour, people had stopped thinking of it as a number that could change. It was just weather.

It became my problem when the nightly window got crowded and the two hours started pushing everything behind it past the morning SLA.

The usual suspects, cleared

First instinct: missing index. No, the scans were on staging tables that got truncated and reloaded nightly; indexing them would just move the cost into the load. Second instinct: bad join order. The plan looked reasonable. Statistics were fresh. No parameter sniffing weirdness. The individual pieces were all fine, which was exactly the clue.

What it actually was

The procedure was written as one majestic statement: five CTEs stacked on top of each other, feeding a final MERGE. It read beautifully. The author clearly (and reasonably) believed the optimizer would materialize each step once, like variables in a script.

That's not what CTEs are. In SQL Server, a CTE is an inline view, a macro, not a checkpoint. Reference it twice and its subtree can execute twice. Stack five of them and the optimizer faces one enormous plan space, takes its best guess, and on tables this size, guesses wrong somewhere. One of the middle CTEs, a windowed dedup over the largest staging table, was being expanded into two branches of the final plan. The most expensive step in the whole job ran twice, and its misestimated cardinality poisoned every join above it.

The fix, which is almost embarrassing

I broke the statement apart: each CTE became an explicit temp table, in order, with the dedup landing in #deduped exactly once. Temp tables have real statistics, so every subsequent step got honest row counts instead of a guess stacked on a guess. The optimizer's job went from "unravel this cathedral" to five small problems in a row.

Runtime: two hours down to under thirty minutes. No new hardware, no schema changes, no vendor ticket. The diff was mostly indentation.

What I actually took from it

Elegance in SQL is a property of the plan, not the text. The five-CTE version was prettier to read; the temp-table version was prettier to execute, and the database only ever sees the second kind of pretty.

The deeper lesson was about "weather" problems. Every long-lived system has a few, numbers everyone has absorbed as facts of nature. They're often the cheapest wins available, precisely because nobody has looked at them in years. When you inherit a system, ask what everyone waits for and treats as normal. Then go read the plan.
