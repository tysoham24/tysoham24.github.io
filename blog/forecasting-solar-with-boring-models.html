Forecasting Solar with Boring Models

July 2026 · 6 min read

Solar forecasting papers are full of transformers, graph networks, and hybrid deep architectures. Solar forecasting in production, at least everywhere I've seen it, is gradient-boosted trees over good features. The gap between those two sentences is worth a post.

The problem, concretely

Given telemetry from a fleet of inverters and a weather forecast, predict each unit's generation over the next hours. The catch is fleet heterogeneity: different panel orientations, shading from a tree that grew, a string that degraded, dust. Two inverters a mile apart under identical irradiance can produce reliably different curves, each unit has a personality.

Why trees keep winning here

The signal is mostly tabular and mostly physics: irradiance, cloud cover, temperature (panels lose efficiency hot), sun angle by hour of year. Gradient boosting eats interactions between tabular features without being told. It's also cheap to retrain, which matters more than people admit, because fleets change monthly, and when a forecast looks wrong at 6 a.m., feature importances give you a debugging thread instead of an attention map to squint at.

The structure that actually moved the needle in my work at a solar company wasn't a fancier architecture. It was an ensemble split: one global model trained across the fleet, capturing shared physics and giving new units something sensible from day one, plus per-device models that learn each unit's quirks once it has history, blended with a plain random forest for variance. Tuned with Optuna. That combination beat persistence baselines by ~35% MAE on our fleet, and the two-tier split is where most of the gain came from, not the tuning.

Respect the baseline

The dirty secret of solar forecasting is that persistence, "tomorrow at noon equals today at noon", is genuinely hard to beat, because weather is autocorrelated and panels are deterministic given irradiance. Any paper or vendor quoting accuracy without a persistence comparison is hiding the denominator. Clear-sky days, everything looks brilliant; the money is entirely in the cloudy tail.

Features over architecture

If I had to rank where the accuracy came from: solar geometry features first (sun elevation and azimuth encode the daily curve almost by themselves), weather-forecast joins second, per-device history third, model choice a distant fourth. We ended with 43 features, and the marginal transformer experiment never justified its serving cost.

None of this argues deep learning has no place, at grid scale, with satellite imagery in the mix, it does. It argues for earning complexity: start from persistence, add geometry, add weather, add per-device structure, and switch architectures only when the boring one plateaus. Mine hasn't yet.
