Data Quality Gates: The Cheapest Reliability Win in Any Pipeline

July 2026 · 5 min read

Most pipeline failures I've dealt with weren't really pipeline failures. The DAG was green. Every task succeeded. And the data was wrong anyway - an upstream feed went stale, or a source started sending duplicates, or half the rows came through with nulls in a column that mattered. The pipeline did its job perfectly. Its job just happened to be delivering garbage.

The fix costs about forty lines of Python.

The idea

A quality gate is a check that runs between fetching data and landing it, and refuses to land a batch that fails. Not a dashboard you look at later. Not a dbt test that fails after the mart is already built. A gate. The batch doesn't get in.

Here's the entire suite from my transit lakehouse project, which ingests live NYC subway positions:

Is the batch empty? Are there duplicate entity IDs? Is the feed's own header timestamp more than five minutes old? Are more than half the rows missing a route ID?

That's it. Four checks. No framework, no YAML, no vendor. Each one returns a human-readable sentence, and if the list isn't empty, the ingestion raises and the orchestrator shows a red run with an error message a human can act on: stale feed: header timestamp lags by 0:12:41.

Why "fail loudly" beats "log and continue"

The tempting alternative is to land the batch anyway and log a warning. Don't. Warnings don't get read; they get grepped, after the damage. If bad data lands in your bronze layer, it flows into every mart, every dashboard, and, worst case, someone's decision, before anyone checks a log. Deleting bad rows out of a lakehouse table is far more painful than re-running one ingestion an hour later.

A failed run, on the other hand, is impossible to ignore. It pages someone, or at least shows up red in the UI. The system's silence becomes trustworthy again: green means good data, not just "the code didn't crash."

Where people overdo it

The failure mode on the other side is buying a data-quality platform before you have data-quality checks. Teams stand up Great Expectations with its store and docs site and checkpoint configs, write three expectations, and never touch it again, the setup cost ate the appetite. The tools are good. But they earn their keep at fifty checks, not five.

Start with a function that takes a DataFrame and returns a list of problems. You will know when you've outgrown it, because the function will be two hundred lines and three teams will want to share it. That's the day to adopt a framework, migration is easy precisely because the checks are already written down.

What to check first

If you only add three gates to an existing pipeline, make them these: volume (row count within an expected band, catches dead feeds and double-sends), freshness (source timestamp, not wall clock, catches the upstream job that silently stopped), and key uniqueness (catches the retry bug that will otherwise cost you an afternoon of deduping). Between them, those three catch most of what actually goes wrong, and each one is a few lines and one loud exception.
